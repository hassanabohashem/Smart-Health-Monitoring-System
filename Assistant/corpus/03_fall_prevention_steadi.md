# CDC STEADI — Stopping Elderly Accidents, Deaths, and Injuries

> **Scope: this document targets adults aged 65 and older.** Fall prevention for younger adults, children, and infants follows different principles (e.g. baby-proofing for toddlers, sports safety for adolescents).

Falls are the leading cause of injury and injury-related death for adults 65 and older. One in four older adults falls each year. The CDC STEADI initiative is the primary evidence-based fall prevention program in the United States for this age group.

## Why falls matter in elderly patients
- Approximately 36 million falls occur among older adults each year in the United States.
- Falls cause about 3 million emergency department visits and over 32,000 deaths annually.
- Hip fractures: about 95 percent are caused by falling. One in three adults over 50 who sustain a hip fracture dies within 12 months.
- Fear of falling after an initial fall can lead to activity restriction, muscle weakness, and paradoxically more falls.

## Three-step STEADI screening

### Step 1: Screen
Three key questions establish fall risk:
1. Have you fallen in the past year?
2. Do you feel unsteady when standing or walking?
3. Do you worry about falling?
If any answer is yes, the patient is at increased fall risk.

### Step 2: Assess
For patients screened positive, assess:
- Gait, strength, and balance (Timed Up and Go test — concerning if 12 seconds or more).
- Orthostatic hypotension (measure BP lying and standing).
- Medications (see high-risk medication list below).
- Vision (has the patient had an eye exam in the past year?).
- Home hazards (loose rugs, poor lighting, lack of grab bars).
- Feet and footwear.
- Vitamin D status.

### Step 3: Intervene
Recommended interventions include:
- Physical therapy referral for strength and balance training. Tai chi and Otago programs are evidence-based.
- Medication review and deprescribing where possible.
- Vitamin D 800 IU per day if deficient or at risk.
- Home safety assessment: grab bars in bathroom, removal of throw rugs, improved lighting, stair rails.
- Correction of vision and proper footwear.
- Orthostatic blood pressure management: hydration, gradual position changes, review of antihypertensives.

## Medications commonly implicated in falls
- Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs: zolpidem, diazepam, lorazepam, alprazolam.
- Opioids: oxycodone, hydrocodone, tramadol, morphine.
- First-generation antihistamines: diphenhydramine, doxylamine.
- Antipsychotics: haloperidol, quetiapine, risperidone.
- Antidepressants, especially tricyclics (amitriptyline) and to a lesser extent SSRIs and SNRIs.
- Skeletal muscle relaxants: cyclobenzaprine.
- Some antihypertensives when they cause orthostatic hypotension, especially alpha-blockers (doxazosin).
- Diuretics if causing volume depletion.
- Anticonvulsants.

Consider discussing with the prescriber whether the dose can be reduced or the medication can be changed to a lower-risk alternative.

## After a fall — red flags for emergency services
Call emergency services if any of the following is true after a fall:
- Head struck the ground or any hard surface.
- Patient is on blood thinners (warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban, clopidogrel, aspirin).
- Loss of consciousness, even briefly.
- Inability to bear weight or move a limb.
- Severe pain in hip, back, or wrist (common fracture sites).
- Confusion after the fall or worsening over hours.
- Vomiting or severe headache after a fall.

## Prevention at home — practical checklist
- Install grab bars in bathrooms and non-slip mats in the tub and shower.
- Remove throw rugs or secure them firmly to the floor.
- Improve lighting, especially in hallways, stairs, and the path to the bathroom.
- Keep floors clear of clutter and cables.
- Wear supportive shoes with firm soles, not slippers or loose socks.
- Use a cane or walker if prescribed, and use it correctly.
- Do not rush when getting up from bed or a chair — sit for a moment, then stand slowly.

Source: Centers for Disease Control and Prevention STEADI (Stopping Elderly Accidents, Deaths, and Injuries) toolkit.
