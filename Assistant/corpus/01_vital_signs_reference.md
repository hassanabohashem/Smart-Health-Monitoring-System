# Vital Signs Reference — by age group

Heart rate, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and body temperature each have age-dependent normal ranges. Wearers of the Smart Health platform can be any age — the thresholds below cover infants through older adults.

## Heart rate (resting, awake)
Normal resting heart rate depends on age.

| Age group | Normal range (bpm) |
|---|---|
| Newborn (0–3 months) | 100–160 |
| Infant (3 months–1 year) | 80–140 |
| Toddler (1–3 years) | 75–130 |
| Preschooler (3–6 years) | 75–120 |
| School age (6–12 years) | 70–115 |
| Adolescent (12–18 years) | 60–100 |
| Adult (18–64) | 60–100 |
| Older adult (65+) | 60–100 |

Notes that apply across ages:
- Well-conditioned adolescents, adults, and older adults may have resting heart rates as low as 50 without symptoms.
- Medications such as beta-blockers, calcium-channel blockers, and digoxin frequently produce asymptomatic adult heart rates of 45 to 60.
- A heart rate persistently above the normal range warrants investigation for fever, dehydration, anemia, pain, thyroid disease, or drug effect.
- A heart rate below the crit-low threshold (40 in adults; higher in children) with symptoms — fainting, severe weakness, chest pain — is an emergency.

## Oxygen saturation (SpO2)
Normal range is 95 to 100 percent across all ages.

- Healthy non-smoking individuals should maintain 95 percent or above at rest.
- Patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) often have a baseline of 88 to 92 percent. This is their "new normal" and is acceptable when the patient is at baseline.
- An acute drop of 3 or more percentage points from the patient's personal baseline is clinically significant regardless of the absolute number.
- SpO2 below 90 percent indicates hypoxia and usually requires supplemental oxygen.
- SpO2 below 85 percent is a medical emergency at any age.

## Respiratory rate
Normal respiratory rate varies with age.

| Age group | Normal range (breaths/min) |
|---|---|
| Newborn | 30–60 |
| Infant | 20–40 |
| Toddler | 20–30 |
| Preschooler | 20–30 |
| School age | 18–25 |
| Adolescent | 12–20 |
| Adult | 12–20 |

Tachypnea (rate above the upper limit) often precedes overt clinical deterioration by hours and is one of the earliest signs of sepsis or respiratory failure at any age. Bradypnea (rate below the lower limit) is especially concerning in patients taking opioids or benzodiazepines.

## Body temperature
Normal oral/core temperature range is 36.1 to 37.9 °C (97 to 100 °F) across all ages.

Special considerations:
- **Newborns and infants under 3 months**: any rectal temperature above 38 °C is a medical emergency and warrants immediate evaluation. Infants cannot regulate temperature well and a fever may be the only sign of serious infection.
- **Older adults**: often fail to mount a strong febrile response. A temperature of 37.8 °C in an older adult may represent significant infection where 39 °C would be expected in a younger adult.
- Hypothermia (temperature below 35 °C) is dangerous at any age. Common causes include sepsis, hypothyroidism, medication effects, and environmental exposure.

## When to call emergency services — any age
- Chest pain or pressure lasting more than a few minutes (adults and adolescents)
- Sudden severe shortness of breath
- Sudden weakness or numbness on one side of the body
- Sudden difficulty speaking or understanding speech
- Sudden severe headache with no known cause
- Unresponsiveness or inability to wake the patient
- Severe uncontrolled bleeding
- Fall with apparent head injury or inability to move a limb
- Any fever in an infant under 3 months

Sources: American Heart Association vital-sign references; Pediatric Advanced Life Support; WHO ICOPE; NIA fact sheets.
