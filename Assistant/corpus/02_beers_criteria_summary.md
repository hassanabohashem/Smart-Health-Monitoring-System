# AGS 2023 Beers Criteria — Potentially Inappropriate Medications for Older Adults

> **Scope: this document applies specifically to adults aged 65 and older.** Beers Criteria thresholds are not validated for younger adults or children. The medications discussed here can still be appropriate in younger patients when indicated.

The American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria identifies medications that are potentially inappropriate in older adults because the risks outweigh the benefits in this age group. The 2023 update is the most recent. Below are the high-impact categories relevant to daily caregiver decisions for patients 65+.

## Medications generally to avoid in adults 65 years and older

### First-generation antihistamines
Examples: diphenhydramine (Benadryl), doxylamine (Unisom), chlorpheniramine.
Rationale: highly anticholinergic. In the elderly they cause confusion, dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, and greatly increase fall risk. Cumulative anticholinergic exposure is associated with cognitive decline and dementia.
Alternatives: second-generation antihistamines (loratadine, cetirizine) if allergy treatment needed; sleep hygiene measures rather than OTC sleep aids.

### Benzodiazepines
Examples: diazepam (Valium), lorazepam (Ativan), alprazolam (Xanax), clonazepam (Klonopin), triazolam (Halcion).
Rationale: elderly metabolize these more slowly. Risk of cognitive impairment, delirium, falls, fractures, and motor vehicle accidents. Withdrawal must be gradual.
Alternatives: cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia; SSRIs for anxiety; melatonin for sleep. Never stop benzodiazepines abruptly.

### Z-drugs (non-benzodiazepine sedative-hypnotics)
Examples: zolpidem (Ambien), zaleplon (Sonata), eszopiclone (Lunesta).
Rationale: adverse effects similar to benzodiazepines in the elderly: falls, delirium, motor vehicle accidents. Chronic use is strongly discouraged.

### Long-acting sulfonylureas
Examples: glyburide (Diabeta, Micronase), chlorpropamide.
Rationale: high risk of severe and prolonged hypoglycemia in older adults. Hypoglycemia in elderly can cause falls, confusion, and fatal cardiovascular events.
Preferred: glipizide (shorter-acting), DPP-4 inhibitors, or SGLT2 inhibitors where appropriate.

### Muscle relaxants
Examples: cyclobenzaprine (Flexeril), carisoprodol (Soma), methocarbamol (Robaxin), orphenadrine.
Rationale: anticholinergic, sedating, associated with falls and confusion. Poorly tolerated in the elderly.
Alternatives: physical therapy, topical analgesics, acetaminophen.

### Tertiary-amine tricyclic antidepressants
Examples: amitriptyline (Elavil), imipramine (Tofranil), doxepin at doses over 6 mg.
Rationale: highly anticholinergic and highly sedating. Avoid in elderly.
Alternatives: SSRIs, SNRIs, or nortriptyline if a tricyclic is truly needed.

### NSAIDs (chronic use)
Examples: ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin), naproxen (Aleve), diclofenac, meloxicam, celecoxib.
Rationale: increase risk of gastrointestinal bleeding, acute kidney injury, and heart failure exacerbation. Occasional short-term use may be acceptable; chronic use is to be avoided.
Alternatives: acetaminophen (up to 3 g/day in elderly without liver disease), topical NSAIDs, physical therapy.

### Digoxin above 0.125 mg/day
Rationale: elderly patients with reduced kidney function are prone to digoxin toxicity. Symptoms include nausea, visual disturbances (yellow halos), arrhythmias.

## Drug-disease and drug-drug interactions to avoid

### Medications worsening heart failure
NSAIDs, non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem in reduced EF), thiazolidinediones (pioglitazone).

### Medications causing or worsening delirium
Anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, corticosteroids, H2-receptor antagonists (cimetidine, ranitidine), meperidine.

### Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) beyond 8 weeks
Examples: omeprazole (Prilosec), esomeprazole (Nexium), pantoprazole (Protonix).
Long-term use is associated with Clostridium difficile infection, bone loss and fractures, vitamin B12 deficiency, and magnesium deficiency. Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration possible.

## Cumulative anticholinergic burden
Many common medications have anticholinergic effects: diphenhydramine, oxybutynin, tolterodine, amitriptyline, paroxetine, cyclobenzaprine, hyoscyamine. When two or more are combined, the cumulative burden substantially increases the risk of confusion, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, and cognitive decline. The Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden (ACB) score is a validated tool.

## Key caveats
- The Beers Criteria is guidance, not a prohibition. Clinical judgment and individualized care still apply.
- Always involve the prescriber. Do not stop medications without consultation.
- Deprescribing is the deliberate process of reducing or stopping medications that may no longer be beneficial. It should be done gradually and with monitoring.

Source: American Geriatrics Society Beers Criteria 2023 update, J Am Geriatr Soc.
