# Common Chronic Conditions — recognition and red flags

This document summarizes common chronic conditions that a wearable health monitor might surface warning signs for, across age groups. Each section notes the typical age profile and provides caregiver-level information on recognition, warning signs, and when to seek urgent care.

## Heart failure
Heart failure occurs when the heart cannot pump blood efficiently enough to meet the body's needs. Most common in adults over 70 (~10% prevalence) but also occurs in younger adults with cardiomyopathies or viral myocarditis, and in children with congenital heart disease. Pediatric presentation differs: infants may show poor feeding or failure to thrive; children show exercise intolerance.

### Warning signs of worsening heart failure
- Shortness of breath, especially when lying flat or at night
- Swelling of the feet, ankles, or legs
- Rapid weight gain (2 kg or 4 pounds in a week is a warning sign)
- Persistent cough, sometimes with pink-tinged sputum
- Fatigue with usual activities
- Racing or irregular heartbeat

### When to call emergency services
- Severe shortness of breath at rest, especially if leaning forward
- Chest pain or pressure
- Coughing up pink or bloody frothy sputum
- Confusion or blue-tinged lips

### Daily management
- Weigh every morning at the same time. A gain of 1 kg in one day or 2 kg in a week should prompt a call to the doctor.
- Restrict salt intake to under 2 g per day (one teaspoon).
- Take medications as prescribed — especially diuretics, ACE inhibitors/ARBs, beta-blockers, and SGLT2 inhibitors.
- Gentle daily activity as tolerated.

## Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)
COPD causes progressive airflow obstruction and is the third leading cause of death worldwide.

### Expected findings in a stable COPD patient
- Baseline SpO2 often 88 to 92 percent at rest. This is their personal normal.
- Mild shortness of breath with exertion.
- Chronic cough, sometimes with sputum.

### Signs of an acute exacerbation
- Increased shortness of breath at rest or with minimal exertion
- Change in sputum color to yellow or green (suggesting infection)
- Increased cough intensity or frequency
- Chest tightness
- Increased use of rescue inhaler
- SpO2 dropping more than 3 to 4 percentage points below personal baseline
- Fever

### When to seek emergency care
- Severe shortness of breath that is not relieved by the rescue inhaler
- Confusion, drowsiness, or difficulty speaking in full sentences
- Blue lips or fingertips
- Chest pain

## Diabetes — types 1 and 2
About 25 percent of adults over 65 have type 2 diabetes; type 1 diabetes often begins in childhood or young adulthood and requires lifelong insulin. Both types share many emergency considerations.

### Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) — dangerous at any age
Hypoglycemia can cause falls, confusion, and cardiovascular events. In children it can present as irritability, pallor, or unusual sleepiness. In older adults it can present subtly as confusion without the classic sweating. In anyone, severe hypoglycemia causes loss of consciousness or seizures.
Symptoms: sweating, shakiness, dizziness, confusion, irritability, pale appearance.
Immediate treatment: if the patient is conscious and can swallow, give 15 g of fast-acting carbohydrate (4 glucose tablets, 1/2 cup juice, 1 tablespoon sugar or honey). Recheck blood glucose in 15 minutes. If still low, repeat. If unconscious, call emergency services immediately — do not give anything by mouth.

### Hyperglycemia
Symptoms: excessive thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision. Prolonged elevation can progress to hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state, which is a medical emergency in elderly patients (altered mental status, severe dehydration).

### Glycemic goals by patient group
- Children and adults up to ~65: HbA1c target 7.0 percent or lower (individualized).
- Most healthy older adults (65+): HbA1c 7.0 to 7.5 percent.
- Frail older adults or those with limited life expectancy: HbA1c 7.5 to 8.5 percent to avoid hypoglycemia.

## Osteoporosis
> Primarily affects adults 50+, especially postmenopausal women and men over 70. Rare in children except in secondary causes (steroid use, juvenile idiopathic osteoporosis).

Osteoporosis increases fracture risk, particularly hip, spine, and wrist.

### Prevention and management
- Calcium 1000 to 1200 mg/day from diet and supplements combined.
- Vitamin D 800 to 2000 IU per day.
- Weight-bearing exercise (walking, light resistance training).
- Bone density scan (DEXA) every 2 years after age 65 for women, age 70 for men.
- Medications: bisphosphonates (alendronate, risedronate), denosumab, or anabolic agents for established osteoporosis.

## Dementia and cognitive decline
> Primarily affects adults 65+. Over 10 percent of adults over 65 have some form of dementia; over 30 percent of those over 85.

### Warning signs (beyond normal age-related memory changes)
- Getting lost in familiar places
- Difficulty managing finances, medications, or appointments
- Major changes in personality or judgment
- Difficulty with language (word-finding beyond normal)
- Poor hygiene or self-neglect

### Sudden cognitive change = medical emergency
A sudden change in mental status in an elderly patient is delirium until proven otherwise. Causes include:
- Urinary tract infection
- Pneumonia or any infection
- Dehydration
- Medication effect, especially anticholinergics and sedatives
- Stroke
- Low blood sugar
- Constipation with fecal impaction
Always call the doctor or emergency services for acute confusion in an elderly person.

## Chronic kidney disease
Kidney function declines with age — by 75, most adults have moderately reduced kidney function. Pediatric CKD is less common and usually has identifiable causes (congenital anomalies, glomerulonephritis).

### Implications
- Many medications require dose adjustment: metformin, gabapentin, most antibiotics, digoxin, direct oral anticoagulants.
- NSAIDs should generally be avoided.
- Contrast for imaging may temporarily worsen function.
- Monitor for fluid retention (swelling, shortness of breath).

## Depression — across ages
Depression affects all ages and presents differently by life stage:
- **Children**: irritability, drop in school performance, somatic complaints, withdrawal.
- **Adolescents**: irritability, social withdrawal, sleep changes, substance use, self-harm risk.
- **Adults**: classic symptoms — sad mood, anhedonia, sleep/appetite changes, hopelessness.
- **Older adults**: somatic complaints (pain, fatigue, poor sleep, poor appetite) may dominate over sadness. Depression is not a normal part of aging and is treatable.

### Warning signs
- Withdrawal from usual activities
- Statements of hopelessness
- Thoughts of death or suicide (always ask directly; asking does not increase risk)
- Weight loss or gain
- Severe insomnia or hypersomnia
- Self-neglect

Sources: NIA NIH fact sheets, GOLD COPD guidelines, ADA Standards of Medical Care in Diabetes, AACE osteoporosis guidelines.
