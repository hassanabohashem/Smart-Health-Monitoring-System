# Daily Wellness — Hydration, Nutrition, Exercise, Sleep

Applies across ages. Specific age-group guidance is called out where it differs.

## Hydration
Dehydration is common and often missed in both children and older adults. The thirst sensation is weaker in both groups, and medications like diuretics and conditions like uncontrolled diabetes accelerate fluid loss.

### Daily fluid targets (all fluids — water, milk, soup, juice)
- Infants under 6 months: exclusive breast milk or formula (no extra water needed)
- Toddlers (1–3 y): about 1 L per day
- Children (4–8 y): about 1.2 L per day
- Children (9–13 y): 1.6–1.8 L
- Adolescents and adults: 2.0–2.7 L (higher in active individuals)
- Older adults: 1.5–2 L (adjust down for heart failure to ~1.5 L per doctor's instructions)
- Hot weather, fever, or physical activity increase needs at every age.

### Signs of dehydration
**Across ages:**
- Dark-colored urine or less frequent urination
- Dry mouth or dry tongue
- Low blood pressure, rapid heart rate
- Reduced skin turgor (skin "tenting" when pinched)
- Sudden weight loss (1 kg can reflect 1 L of fluid)

**In older adults specifically:** confusion, dizziness, or lethargy may be the FIRST signs — they can appear before any classical dehydration signs.

**In infants and young children:** fewer wet diapers than usual, no tears when crying, sunken eyes, sunken fontanelle (the soft spot on the head), unusual sleepiness or irritability.

Severe dehydration can cause acute kidney injury, delirium, falls, and hospitalization. If the elderly person is confused or cannot hold down fluids, seek medical care.

## Nutrition
Nutritional needs vary by age and life stage.

### Protein
- Children (growth phase): ~1.0 g/kg body weight per day.
- Healthy adults: ~0.8 g/kg/day (more for athletes or pregnant women).
- Older adults (65+): 1.0 to 1.2 g/kg — more than younger adults, to counter sarcopenia.
- Good sources: eggs, fish, chicken, lentils, beans, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, milk (age-appropriate).

### Key micronutrients by age
- **Infants & children**: iron, vitamin D (especially in breastfed infants), calcium for growing bones.
- **Adolescents**: iron (especially menstruating girls), calcium (peak bone mass is built before age 30).
- **Pregnant/lactating women**: folate, iron, iodine, DHA.
- **Older adults**: vitamin B12 (absorption declines with age and PPI use), vitamin D, calcium 1000–1200 mg/day, fiber 25–30 g/day to prevent constipation.

### Warning signs of undernutrition (any age)
- Unintentional weight loss (more than 5 percent in 6 months)
- Poor wound healing
- Frequent infections
- Fatigue and weakness
- In children: failure to thrive, failure to meet growth milestones
- In older adults: loss of muscle mass (sarcopenia)

## Physical activity
Activity improves mood, cardiovascular health, and (in older adults) cognition and fall prevention.

### WHO activity recommendations
- **Children and adolescents (5–17)**: at least 60 minutes per day of moderate-to-vigorous activity, plus muscle-strengthening activities 3x/week.
- **Adults (18–64)**: at least 150 minutes per week of moderate activity OR 75 minutes of vigorous, plus muscle-strengthening 2x/week.
- **Older adults (65+)**: same as adults if able, plus balance training (tai chi, standing balance) at least 3x/week to prevent falls.
- **Frail older adults**: even 10 minutes of gentle activity daily is beneficial. Something is always better than nothing.

## Sleep
Sleep needs vary dramatically across life stages.

### Healthy sleep targets per age (National Sleep Foundation)
- Newborns (0–3 months): 14–17 hours total (including daytime naps)
- Infants (4–11 months): 12–15 hours total
- Toddlers (1–2 y): 11–14 hours total
- Preschoolers (3–5 y): 10–13 hours total
- School-age children (6–13 y): 9–11 hours
- Teenagers (14–17 y): 8–10 hours
- Young adults and adults: 7–9 hours
- Older adults (65+): 7–8 hours

### Notes
- Short naps (under 30 minutes, early afternoon) are generally fine for adults and older adults.
- Long daytime naps or napping in the late afternoon may worsen nighttime sleep.
- Sleep architecture changes with age — older adults often wake more during the night. This does not always indicate a problem.

### Sleep hygiene
- Consistent bedtime and wake time
- Morning sunlight exposure
- Avoid caffeine after midday and alcohol before bed
- Limit screens before bedtime
- Comfortable, dark, cool bedroom
- Avoid heavy meals close to bedtime

### Warning signs requiring evaluation
- Loud snoring with pauses in breathing (possible sleep apnea)
- Excessive daytime sleepiness despite adequate night sleep
- Acting out dreams physically (may suggest REM sleep behavior disorder, linked to Parkinson's)
- Insomnia lasting more than 3 weeks

### About sleep medications
**Do not give over-the-counter sleep aids to children** without medical guidance — many contain first-generation antihistamines (diphenhydramine, doxylamine) which are not appropriate for pediatric use. In **older adults** these same ingredients are Beers-listed because of confusion, falls, and cognitive decline risk. Melatonin is generally safer across ages but has only modest benefit. If sleep problems persist, cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) is the first-line recommendation for adolescents and adults.

## Social connection
Loneliness and social isolation are associated with increased mortality, depression, and cognitive decline across all ages — including adolescents, for whom social connectedness is a key mental-health protective factor. For older adults living alone, regular check-ins by family members, phone calls, community centers, and religious communities all provide valuable social support.

Source: NIA NIH caregiver fact sheets, USDA dietary guidelines for older adults, WHO ICOPE.
